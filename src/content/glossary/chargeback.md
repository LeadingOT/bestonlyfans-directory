# What is Chargeback

**A chargeback is a forced transaction reversal initiated by a customer's bank or credit card issuer, typically to resolve a dispute over a fraudulent or unsatisfactory purchase.**

A chargeback is a consumer protection mechanism embedded in the payment card networks (like Visa, Mastercard, and American Express). It allows a cardholder to dispute a charge directly with their issuing bank, rather than the merchant, if they believe the transaction is erroneous or illegitimate. The bank then investigates the claim and, if it finds merit, withdraws the funds from the merchant's account and returns them to the cardholder. This process is distinct from a standard refund, which is a voluntary agreement between the customer and the merchant.

Common reasons for chargebacks include fraudulent transactions (where the card was used without the owner's consent), goods or services not being delivered as described, technical issues like double billing, and failure to process an agreed-upon refund. In the context of digital content and subscription services, chargebacks often occur when a customer forgets about a recurring payment, feels the content did not match its description, or encounters difficulties canceling a subscription.

For merchants, particularly in industries like content creation, chargebacks are critical to understand because they directly impact revenue and operational health. Each chargeback results in a lost sale, the forfeiture of any associated fees, and often an additional penalty fee from the payment processor. A high chargeback rate can lead to increased processing costs, the loss of the ability to accept card payments, and damage to the business's reputation. For consumers, while chargebacks are a vital safeguard against fraud, they should be used responsibly; falsely disputing valid charges (friendly fraud) is a form of abuse that harms merchants.

**Related terms:** Refund, Payment Dispute, Merchant Account, Fraud Prevention, Payment Processor.
